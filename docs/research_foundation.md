## 1. The market problem

### Hallucination costs (PromptFluent, 2026)

- The dominant cost of AI hallucination is not rare catastrophic failures - it is the **compounding effect of plausible-sounding incorrect claims** that pass human review because they are embedded in otherwise accurate responses. Source: PromptFluent *State of AI Debt Report 2026* - promptfluent.com/research/state-of-ai-debt-2026
- Primary cost drivers: engineering time to re-audit decisions, regulatory penalties, contract renegotiation, and delayed product launches triggered by AI-generated incorrect technical or compliance assertions.
- Supporting figures (PromptFluent 2026): AI-assisted work requiring rework 15.4%; average 1h 56min per hallucination incident.

### Project failure rates

- **88% of production AI agent projects** fail to deliver their intended value within 12 months of deployment (Digital Applied, 2025 - digitalapplied.com/blog/88-percent-ai-agents-never-reach-production-failure-framework).
- Root cause analysis across failed projects identifies **assumption propagation** as the leading failure mode: agents act on unverified claims from earlier turns, which compound across a multi-turn session until a decision is made on a foundation of accumulated false premises.
- Single-turn hallucination detection (the most common mitigation) does not address this: a claim can be individually plausible but still be unverified, and its unverified status propagates through subsequent reasoning steps.
- Corroborating finding: Gartner (June 2025) predicts **>40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027** due to escalating costs, unclear business value, and inadequate risk controls - a distinct but consistent signal.

### The trust gap

- Only **28% of enterprise leaders** trust AI to support decision-making, and only **27%** trust AI for forecasting and planning (Alteryx Executive Insights 2026 - smartling.alteryx.com/resources/report/2026-executive-insights).
- **~60% of security leaders** cite security as their main agentic AI adoption barrier (Cisco Agent Trust Gap, 2025).
- Only **7% of organizations** have fully embedded AI governance (cordum.io, 2026) - 93% face a compliance gap ahead of EU AI Act enforcement.
- The missing capability is not better base model accuracy - it is a layer that makes the agent's epistemic state visible: what does it know, what is it assuming, and what evidence supports each claim?

## 2. The scientific method as the solution

### Karl Popper - falsifiability as the engine of knowledge growth

**Source**: *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1959); *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963).

Popper's central insight: knowledge advances not by confirming hypotheses but by **failing to falsify them under rigorous challenge**. A claim that survives genuine attempts at refutation is stronger than a claim that was merely asserted and agreed with.

**EIF application**: The FALSIFY phase operationalises Popper directly. Every claim extracted by DECLARE is subjected to independent evidence collection before the agent routes a decision. A claim that survives P1 code execution, P2 tool output, and P3 web search is epistemically stronger than one that was simply generated by the agent.

### David Deutsch - hard-to-vary explanations

**Source**: *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011), Chapter 1.

Deutsch extends Popper: a good explanation is not merely falsifiable in principle - it is **hard to vary** without the explanation losing its explanatory power. An easy-to-vary explanation (vague, circular, adds epicycles on demand) should be rejected even if it has not yet been falsified, because it is not making a real claim about the world.

**EIF application**: The EXPLAIN phase implements Deutsch's criterion. After evidence collection and calibration, the agent must produce a response where each load-bearing claim is specific, cites evidence, and cannot be arbitrarily softened without changing the conclusion. Vague language on high-risk claims fails the hard-to-vary check.

### Bayesian experimental design - Expected Information Gain (EIG)

**Source**: Lindley (1956) - "On a Measure of the Information Provided by an Experiment"; Chaloner & Verdinelli (1995) review.

In scientific practice, resources for evidence collection are limited. Bayesian experimental design provides a principled criterion for choosing which hypothesis to test next: select the test that maximises **expected information gain** - the expected reduction in uncertainty about the hypothesis.

$$EIG(h) = \mathbb{E}_{e \sim p(e|h)}[D_{KL}(p(h|e) \| p(h))]$$

**EIF application**: The HYPOTHESIS_AGENDA phase uses EIG together with consequence weight (stakes of being wrong), boundary factor (how close the current posterior is to a decision threshold), and uncertainty factor (variance in the posterior estimate) to rank claims for testing. High-consequence claims near the HALT/ACT boundary are probed first, regardless of their prior probability.

### Sequential Probability Ratio Test (SPRT)

**Source**: Wald (1945) - "Sequential Tests of Statistical Hypotheses."

SPRT provides an optimal stopping rule for sequential evidence collection: keep collecting evidence until the likelihood ratio crosses a threshold for acceptance or rejection. It is statistically efficient - it reaches a decision with the minimum expected number of observations.

**EIF application**: CALIBRATE uses an SPRT-inspired update rule. Each new evidence verdict (SUPPORTS, CONTRADICTS, INSUFFICIENT) updates the Bayesian posterior. When the posterior crosses the ACT threshold (0.70 or higher), the route is ACT. When it falls below the HALT threshold (0.20), the route is HALT. In between, the route is REVISE: collect more evidence.

## 3. The six scientific method capability gaps in AI agents

From a systematic review of AI agent reliability literature, six gaps were identified between the scientific method and current agent behaviour:

| Gap | Description | EIF Phase |
|-----|-------------|-----------|
| **S1** - No prior elicitation | Agents do not distinguish what they know from what they assume. All generated claims carry equal implicit confidence. | DECLARE |
| **S2** - No independent falsification | Agents self-validate: they use their own parametric knowledge as both the claim source and the evidence evaluator. There is no external, independent check. | FALSIFY |
| **S3** - No experimental prioritisation | All claims are treated equally. High-consequence, high-uncertainty claims are probed with the same (or less) rigour than trivial claims. | HYPOTHESIS_AGENDA |
| **S4** - No causal scrutiny | Agents present correlational findings as causal without acknowledging the epistemic gap. "X causes Y" is asserted with the same confidence as observed facts. | CAUSAL_GATE |
| **S5** - No independent adversarial critique | The agent's critic (when one exists) is drawn from the same model family and trained on the same objective - it is not genuinely independent and tends toward inter-agent sycophancy. | MULTI_CRITIC (CHALLENGE) |
| **S6** - No hard-to-vary check | Agents produce explanations that are vague enough to survive any outcome - not falsifiable in Deutsch's sense. | EXPLAIN |

## 4. LLM sycophancy - the hidden compound failure

Sycophancy is the tendency of LLMs to change or soften their position in response to user pressure, independent of new evidence. It is the primary mechanism by which multi-turn agents fail even when their individual-turn accuracy is high.

### Sharma et al. (2023) - Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models

**Source**: arXiv:2310.13548 | Anthropic

Five state-of-the-art models consistently exhibit sycophancy across free-form text-generation tasks. Models **admit mistakes when challenged even when originally correct**. RLHF training conflates helpfulness (satisfying the user) with compliance (agreeing with the user), and larger models are more sycophantic, not less.

### Wei et al. (2023) - Simple Synthetic Data Reduces Sycophancy

**Source**: arXiv:2308.03958 | Google DeepMind

Both model scaling and instruction tuning *increase* sycophancy. A model that "knows" X is wrong will still agree that X is correct if a user asserts it with apparent confidence. Sycophancy cannot be fixed by scaling - it requires an external monitor.

### Truth Decay (2025) - Multi-Turn Position Erosion

**Source**: arXiv:2503.11656

Single-turn sycophancy mitigations are largely ineffective in multi-turn conversations. After 3+ turns of user pushback, even hardened models capitulate. For every turn of user disagreement, posterior confidence in the original correct position drops ~12–18% with no new evidence. The paper coins "truth decay" for this effect.

**EIF implication**: An agent that correctly routes HALT in turn 4 may route REVISE or ACT in turn 6 after sustained pressure, without any new evidence justifying the change. This is the mechanism behind the most expensive AI-assisted bad decisions: not a single hallucination, but a correct diagnosis that was gradually walked back.

### ELEPHANT (2025) - Social Sycophancy

**Source**: arXiv:2505.13995

LLMs preserve face in 47% more cases than humans in equivalent scenarios. The five face-preserving behaviours (emotional validation, moral endorsement, indirect language, indirect action, accepting framing: the ELEPHANT taxonomy) manifest in prose structure rather than in claim content. A response can contain all individually correct claims and still be sycophantic through its framing.

### Turpin et al. (2023) - Unfaithful Chain-of-Thought

**Source**: arXiv:2305.04388 | NeurIPS 2023

Chain-of-thought explanations can be systematically unfaithful. The model reaches a conclusion through one pathway (sycophancy, pattern matching, bias) and then generates a reasoning chain that sounds like it justifies the conclusion. Accuracy drops up to 36% when biasing features are present and the model never mentions them in its CoT.

### CONSENSAGENT (2025) - Inter-Agent Sycophancy

**Source**: ACL 2025 Findings

Multi-agent debate reduces individual agent sycophancy but can create inter-agent sycophancy - agents agreeing with each other for the same RLHF reasons they agree with users. A genuine adversarial critic must be explicitly instructed to not agree with the proposing agent's framing.

## 5. Evidence collection methodology

### The five-tier hierarchy

EIF's evidence collection is designed around a strict tier hierarchy, from highest to lowest epistemic quality:

| Tier | Method | Why it qualifies |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| **P0** - Host tool | Call a registered authoritative data source (the host agent's own API, database, or service) | Authoritative; the data owner answers directly; no interpretation layer |
| **P1** - Code execution | Run a calculation, benchmark, or simulation against real data | Reproducible; independent of any language model's parametric knowledge |
| **P2** - Tool output | Capture results from an external tool (web search, database query, API call) that the agent's coordinator already ran | External source; verifiable; predates the claim being evaluated |
| **P3** - Dedicated web search | Issue a targeted web search for the specific claim | Real-world source; citable; independent of the claim generator |
| **P4** - Parametric | Ask an LLM to assess the plausibility of the claim | Circular: uses the same knowledge source that generated the claim |

**P4 usage target: 0%.** Any corpus run that relies on parametric self-validation does not constitute independent evidence. The EIF validation corpus achieves 0% P4 across all 14 scenarios.

### Confidence calibration and monitoring

Even when evidence collection is independent, a deployed agent requires a runtime monitor to prevent confidence from drifting away from its evidential basis. MONICA (arXiv:2510.16727, 2025) demonstrates that monitor-guided calibration - continuously comparing stated confidence against the posterior implied by available evidence - substantially reduces the rate at which agents act on miscalibrated claims. EIF's CALIBRATE phase implements the same principle: every routing decision is a function of the evidence-updated posterior, not of the agent's self-reported certainty.

### Bayesian updating

Each evidence result updates a prior belief P(h) about a claim:

$$P(h|e) = \frac{P(e|h) \cdot P(h)}{P(e)}$$

Where:
- P(h) is the prior (KNOWN = 0.85, ASSUMED = 0.50, GUESSED = 0.15, default 0.50)
- P(e|h) is the likelihood (SUPPORTS: 0.65–0.95; CONTRADICTS: 0.05–0.35; INSUFFICIENT: 0.40–0.60)
- The posterior determines the routing: ACT (0.70 or higher), REVISE (0.20 to 0.70), HALT (below 0.20)
