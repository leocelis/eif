# Philosophy of Science - Major Frameworks

## Overview

Philosophy of science asks meta-level questions: What makes something science?
What counts as a good explanation? How does science progress? What is the
relationship between theory and evidence?

The four thinkers below define the major positions in the 20th–21st century
debate and remain the unavoidable reference points for anyone thinking seriously
about methodology.

---

## Karl Popper - Falsificationism (1902–1994)

**Core claim**: Science advances by bold conjectures and rigorous attempts to
refute them. A theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable - if there
exists some possible observation that would, if true, show the theory is false.

**Key texts**:
- *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (Logik der Forschung, 1934; English 1959)
- *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963)
- *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945)

**The demarcation problem**: Popper proposed falsifiability as the criterion
separating science from pseudoscience. He compared Einstein's general relativity
(which made precise, risky predictions that could have falsified it - the
bending of starlight around the sun, confirmed in 1919) with Freudian
psychoanalysis and Marxism (which he argued could accommodate any possible
observation and were therefore unfalsifiable).

**The logic of testing**:
```
If hypothesis H is true, then observation O should occur.
Observation O does not occur.
Therefore H is false (modus tollens).
```

This is the only valid logical move in science. The affirmative case
("O occurred, therefore H") commits the fallacy of affirming the consequent - 
many other hypotheses could also produce O.

**Criticisms**:
- **Duhem-Quine thesis**: No hypothesis is tested in isolation; it always comes
  with auxiliary assumptions. A failed prediction may falsify an auxiliary, not
  the core hypothesis. Falsification in practice is therefore always contested.
- **Problem of induction**: Popper tried to eliminate induction from science
  entirely, but many argue that confirmation (evidence raising the probability
  of a hypothesis) is an irreducible part of scientific reasoning.

**Current standing**: Falsifiability remains the working standard in most
scientific fields. However, philosophers recognize it as a necessary but
insufficient criterion - a heuristic, not a sharp algorithm.

Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Karl Popper* (2023 revision).
https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/popper/

## Thomas Kuhn - Paradigm Shifts (1922–1996)

**Core claim**: Science does not progress by smooth accumulation. Instead, it
alternates between long periods of *normal science* (puzzle-solving within an
accepted paradigm) and brief, turbulent *scientific revolutions* (paradigm
replacement).

**Key text**: *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962; 2nd ed. 1970)

**Key concepts**:

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Paradigm | Shared set of assumptions, methods, exemplary problems, and values governing a scientific community |
| Normal science | Research conducted within a paradigm; solving puzzles, not questioning the paradigm |
| Anomaly | A persistent puzzle that the paradigm cannot solve |
| Crisis | Accumulated anomalies destabilize confidence in the paradigm |
| Scientific revolution | The paradigm is replaced by a new one that better handles anomalies |
| Incommensurability | Competing paradigms share no common neutral language or measurement standard; comparison is difficult |

**Historical examples**:
- Ptolemaic → Copernican astronomy
- Phlogiston theory → Oxygen theory of combustion (Lavoisier)
- Newtonian mechanics → Relativistic mechanics (Einstein)
- Continuous inheritance → Mendelian genetics

**The incommensurability thesis**: Scientists in different paradigms do not
merely disagree about answers - they disagree about what counts as a legitimate
question, a valid method, and acceptable evidence. This makes "rational"
paradigm choice philosophically problematic and introduces a sociological element
into scientific change.

**Criticisms**:
- Kuhn was accused of making scientific change irrational or purely sociological.
  He later clarified that paradigm choice involves shared values (accuracy,
  consistency, breadth, simplicity, fruitfulness) even if they don't determine
  a unique outcome.
- The concept of "paradigm" was notoriously imprecise; Masterman (1970) identified
  at least 21 distinct senses in the first edition. Kuhn acknowledged approximately
  22 uses in his own 1970 Postscript, responding to Masterman's analysis.

**Current standing**: Paradigm and paradigm shift have entered common language.
The framework remains the dominant historical lens for understanding major
scientific transitions, even if its philosophical implications remain contested.

Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Thomas Kuhn* (2023 revision).
https://plato.stanford.edu/Entries/thomas-kuhn/

## Imre Lakatos - Research Programmes (1922–1974)

**Core claim**: Scientists do not work with isolated hypotheses but with
structured *research programmes* - hierarchies of theories with a protected
*hard core* and a flexible *protective belt* of auxiliary hypotheses.

**Key text**: *The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes* (1978,
posthumous collection edited by Worrall and Currie). Cambridge University Press.

**Structure of a research programme**:

```
Hard Core (HC)
  - The central, unfalsifiable-by-convention assumptions
  - Defended by a "negative heuristic": do not direct modus tollens at HC
  - Example (Newtonian mechanics): F = ma and the law of gravitation

Protective Belt (PB)
  - Auxiliary hypotheses that take the modus tollens hits
  - Adjusted, refined, or replaced without abandoning HC
  - Example: initial conditions, assumptions about friction, medium resistance

Positive Heuristic
  - A research agenda: what to work on next, what models to build, in what order
```

**Progressive vs. degenerative research programmes**:
- A programme is **progressive** if each new version predicts novel facts and
  some of those novel predictions are confirmed.
- A programme is **degenerative** if each new version is only an ad hoc patch
  to handle the latest disconfirmation, without novel predictive content.

**Why this matters**: Lakatos explains why scientists rationally persist with
a theory despite anomalies (the protective belt absorbs them) while also
explaining when it becomes rational to abandon a programme (when it consistently
degenerates). This provides a historically realistic account of science that
neither Popper's naive falsificationism nor Kuhn's sociological model achieves.

**Relevance today**: The 2025 Springer paper "The Bayesian Research Programme in
the Methodology of Science" (Springer, 2025) applies Lakatos's framework to
Bayesianism, classifying it as a progressive research programme - it keeps
generating solutions to its own problems rather than merely patching failures.

Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Lakatos* (2022 revision).
https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/lakatos/

## Paul Feyerabend - Epistemological Anarchism (1924–1994)

**Core claim**: There is no single methodology that has always driven successful
science. The history of science shows that "anything goes" - scientists have
violated every proposed methodological rule at some point, and this violation
was often productive.

**Key texts**:
- *Against Method: Outline of an Anarchist Theory of Knowledge* (1975)
- *Science in a Free Society* (1978)
- *Farewell to Reason* (1987)

**The argument**:
Feyerabend began as a Popperian but became his sharpest critic. His analysis of
the Copernican revolution showed that Copernicus succeeded partly by *violating*
contemporary methodological rules - he ignored the observational evidence that
favored Ptolemy (the stellar parallax problem was unsolved for another century)
and used ad hoc moves that Popper would have condemned. If science had followed
Popperian falsificationism strictly, heliocentrism would have been abandoned.

**Counter-induction**: Feyerabend argued for the proliferation of hypotheses that
contradict even well-established theories, because this is how scientific
progress actually works. Multiple competing theories develop different facts - 
evidence that is invisible within one theory becomes visible within another.

**Criticisms**:
- "Anything goes" is read as an embrace of pseudoscience. Feyerabend denied
  this; he was making a descriptive claim about method, not a normative one
  about epistemology.
- He was accused of epistemological relativism. He accepted this for
  *methodology* but not for *truth*.

**Current standing**: Feyerabend is taken seriously in two areas:
1. History and sociology of science (his empirical observations about how
   science actually works are largely confirmed)
2. Critique of scientism - the over-extension of scientific authority into
   domains where it is not the only legitimate form of knowledge

Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, *Feyerabend* (2024 revision).
https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/feyerabend/

## Comparison Matrix

| Dimension | Popper | Kuhn | Lakatos | Feyerabend |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Individual hypothesis | Paradigm | Research programme | Science as a whole |
| Demarcation criterion | Falsifiability | Community acceptance | Progressive vs. degenerative | None (rejects demarcation) |
| How science progresses | Conjecture + refutation | Normal science → revolution | Progressive programmes displace degenerative ones | Proliferation + opportunism |
| Role of anomalies | Falsify the hypothesis | Absorbed until crisis | Absorbed by protective belt | May be productively ignored |
| Rationality of science | Fully rational | Partly sociological | Rational with historical realism | Methodological anarchism |
| Key weakness | Duhem-Quine: isolation impossible | Incommensurability: comparison unclear | Programme evaluation may require hindsight | Risks relativism |

## Beyond the Big Four: Recent Philosophical Work (2025–2026)

**Scientific explanation via stabilization** (arXiv 2601.00168, 2026):
Reconceives explanations as adequate when their inferential relations remain
stable under model updates - i.e., when they preserve specified invariants across
transformations. Applicable in quantum, thermodynamic, and relativistic domains.

**Epistemic understanding vs. knowledge accumulation** (EJPS, 2025):
Using modern cosmology as a case study, proposes that scientific progress is
better measured by improved *understanding* - coherence across interconnected
belief networks - than by knowledge accumulation. This challenges both
Popperian corroboration and Kuhnian paradigm metrics.

**Life cycle of scientific principles** (Synthese, 2025):
A template analyzing how principles develop through five stages:
prehistory → elevation → formalization → generalization → challenge.
Applied to Lorentz invariance and the naturalness principle in physics.
Provides a diagnostic tool for assessing whether a principle is mature or ripe
for challenge.

## David Deutsch - Good Explanations (b. 1953)

**Core claim**: A good explanation is one that is hard to vary while still
accounting for what it purports to account for. This is stronger than Popper's
falsifiability - a falsifiable theory can still be a bad explanation if its
details are arbitrary and easily adjusted post-hoc.

**Key texts**:
- *The Fabric of Reality* (1997, Penguin)
- *The Beginning of Infinity: Explanations That Transform the World* (2011, Viking/Penguin)
- "Constructor Theory" (2013). *Synthese*, 190(18), 4331–4359. arXiv:1210.7439.

**Relationship to Popper**: Deutsch extends Popper rather than replacing him.
Falsifiability remains necessary - but insufficient. A good explanation must
also be *specific* (every detail plays a functional role) and *have reach*
(applies beyond the original domain it was designed for).

**Core additions to the epistemological toolkit**:

1. **Hard to vary**: The criterion that replaces vague "simplicity" or
   "elegance" measures. An explanation is good when changing any specific
   detail breaks it. Newton's inverse-square law is hard to vary (change 2 to
   2.01 → wrong predictions). "The gods did it" is easy to vary (any god,
   any mood, any motive → still "works").

2. **Fallibilism over justificationism**: Knowledge does not need justification
   by authority - it needs to survive criticism. No source is authoritative.
   Progress comes from conjectures that survive adversarial testing, not from
   justified beliefs.

3. **Problems are inevitable and soluble**: Failure means insufficient
   knowledge, not inherent impossibility. The correct response to error is not
   "accept uncertainty" but "find a better explanation."

4. **Reach**: Good explanations solve problems beyond their original scope.
   Rules of thumb have no reach - they fail silently outside their fitted
   domain. Universal explanations (e.g., thermodynamics) have unbounded reach.

5. **Parochialism**: Mistaking local regularities for universal laws. The most
   common epistemological error - and the one most relevant to AI agents
   treating training distribution patterns as universal truths.

**Current standing**: Deutsch's framework is the most active extension of
Popperian epistemology in philosophy of science. It is particularly relevant
to AI epistemology because it provides a criterion (hard to vary) that can be
operationalized computationally, unlike vaguer criteria such as "simplicity"
or "beauty."
