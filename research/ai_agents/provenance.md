# Epistemic Provenance, Accountability, and AI-Generated Science

> **Pre-print scope**: DeepTRACE, DeepReviewer 2.0, and the Research Object paper are all
> arXiv pre-prints not yet peer-reviewed. The ISC Global Reporting Standard is a
> consultation document (in progress, 2026). Citation accuracy figures (40–80%)
> should be treated as provisional pending peer review.

## Overview

Scientific knowledge is only as trustworthy as its chain of evidence. When
AI agents generate hypotheses, analyses, and publications, the question "where
did this claim come from, and can it be verified?" becomes structurally harder
to answer. Epistemic provenance - the auditable trail from claim to evidence
to reasoning - is the prerequisite for reproducibility, accountability, and
correction of errors.

This document covers: the provenance problem in AI science, current technical
and governance frameworks, and the emerging global standards.

---

## What Is Epistemic Provenance?

In human science, epistemic provenance is implicit in the research record:

- The hypothesis was generated by researcher R at institution I
- The experiment was conducted with protocol P, equipment E, on date D
- The data is stored at repository X with identifier Y
- The analysis used code C (version V), statistical method M
- The interpretation was reviewed by peers A, B, C

Any future scientist can trace this chain, identify where it might have gone
wrong, and attempt replication.

**When an AI agent does science, this chain breaks in multiple places:**

| Chain link | Human science | AI agent science |
|---|---|---|
| Hypothesis origin | Researcher's reasoning process | Model weights + training data + prompt - opaque |
| Evidence grounding | Citations in text | May be hallucinated or non-existent |
| Reasoning process | Laboratory notebook | Internal activations - not accessible |
| Reviewer identity | Named peer reviewers | Anonymous model evaluation |
| Accountability | Author faces career consequences | No agent faces career consequences |

## The Provenance Problem in LLMs

**Paper**: arXiv:2509.13365 (2025)
*The Provenance Problem: LLMs and the Breakdown of Citation Norms*

### The Structural Issue

Two distinct provenance failures occur in AI-generated science:

**Citation hallucination** (documented by DeepTRACE, arXiv:2509.04499):
LLMs generate text that resembles the patterns of cited scientific writing
without retrieving actual documents. This produces:
- Plausible-looking citations that do not exist
- Citations to real papers that do not contain the claimed claim
- Claims correctly attributed in form but with wrong numbers or mechanisms
These problems are caught by DeepTRACE's eight-dimension audit framework
(citation accuracy 40–80% across current AI research systems).

**Credit attribution failure** (documented by arXiv:2509.13365, Earp et al., 2025;
also published in *Nature Machine Intelligence*): When an LLM generates text
incorporating ideas from sources embedded in its training data, those sources
receive no credit - even if the author discloses AI use and acts in good faith.
This is a novel form of attributional harm that current disclosure frameworks
do not address: the LLM's contribution from any specific training source is
untraceable through its weights.

The author cannot catch citation hallucination without checking every citation
manually. The credit attribution problem is structurally unsolvable at the
individual author level - it requires upstream provenance infrastructure.

### The Scale Implication

At the current rate of AI-assisted research generation, both failure modes
are compounding faster than the infrastructure to detect and correct them
can be built. This is a systemic epistemic risk.

Sources: arXiv:2509.04499 (DeepTRACE); arXiv:2509.13365 (Earp et al., 2025).

## DeepTRACE - Auditing AI Research Systems

**Paper**: arXiv:2509.04499 (2025)
*DeepTRACE: Auditing Deep Research AI Systems for Tracking Reliability Across
Citations and Evidence*

DeepTRACE is the first systematic framework for auditing AI research systems
across eight measurable dimensions:

| Dimension | What is measured |
|---|---|
| Answer accuracy | Is the factual content correct? |
| Source accuracy | Do cited sources exist? |
| Citation accuracy | Does the source contain the claimed information? |
| Claim-evidence alignment | Does the evidence support the specific claim? |
| Reasoning transparency | Is the inferential path from evidence to claim explicit? |
| Uncertainty disclosure | Are confidence levels and limitations disclosed? |
| Coverage completeness | Does the review omit relevant contrary evidence? |
| Temporal currency | Is evidence current or from superseded literature? |

### Findings

Applied to leading AI research systems (including deep research tools from
major AI labs):

- **Citation accuracy: 40–80%** depending on the system and domain
- Most systems produce unsupported statements indistinguishable from supported
  ones
- Reasoning transparency is uniformly low: the path from evidence to conclusion
  is rarely explicit
- Uncertainty disclosure is inconsistent: systems express high confidence on
  poorly-supported claims

**Implication**: Current AI research outputs cannot be trusted as citable
sources without manual verification of every citation and every claim. This
is not a marginal problem - it affects the majority of outputs in some systems.

Source: arXiv:2509.04499 (2025).

## DeepReviewer 2.0 - Traceable AI Peer Review

**Paper**: arXiv:2604.09590 (2026)
*DeepReviewer 2.0: A Traceable Agentic System for Auditable Scientific
Peer Review*

DeepReviewer 2.0 is designed to make AI peer review auditable rather than
opaque. It produces **traceable review packages**:

### Architecture

```
Paper submission
    ↓
Claim extraction: identify all falsifiable claims in the paper
    ↓
Evidence localization: for each claim, identify the evidence that supports it
    ↓
Anchor generation: create bidirectional links between claims and evidence
    ↓
Reasoning chain: explicit inferential path from evidence → claim
    ↓
Review synthesis: overall assessment with all reasoning made explicit
    ↓
Output: traceable review package (claim × evidence × reasoning × conclusion)
```

### Why Traceability Matters

A standard AI review says "this claim is unsupported." A traceable review says:
"Claim C on line 47 states X. The cited source (Author et al., 2023) states Y
on page 3. X and Y are inconsistent because Z." The second is actionable;
the first is not.

Traceability also enables **review auditing**: a meta-reviewer can check the
AI reviewer's own claims about the paper, creating a recursive provenance
structure.

Source: arXiv:2604.09590 (2026).

## Inspectable AI - The Research Object Paradigm

**Paper**: arXiv:2604.11261 (2026)
*Inspectable AI for Science: A Research Object Approach to Generative AI
Governance*

### The Research Object Framework

The Research Object paradigm (developed in the semantic web community for
human science, now extended to AI) treats a scientific investigation as a
structured, navigable artifact:

```
Research Object = {
  inputs: {
    data: [datasets, with versions and DOIs],
    model: [model name, version, configuration],
    prompts: [exact prompts used, with hashes],
    parameters: [temperature, top-p, seed if fixed]
  },
  process: {
    interactions: [full interaction log with timestamps],
    intermediate_outputs: [all intermediate reasoning steps]
  },
  outputs: {
    claims: [list of claims, with confidence and evidence],
    artifacts: [generated text, code, figures]
  },
  provenance: {
    human_decisions: [which steps involved human judgment],
    ai_decisions: [which steps were fully automated],
    review_history: [who reviewed what, when]
  }
}
```

**Key properties**:
- **Inspectable**: Any component can be examined independently
- **Reproducible**: Given the same inputs and configuration, outputs can be
  regenerated
- **Integrity-preserving**: Modifications are tracked with diffs; original
  is always accessible
- **Confidentiality-compatible**: Sensitive data can be excluded from public
  version while maintaining structural auditability

### Implementation Challenge

Current AI systems do not natively produce Research Objects. They produce text.
Building Research Object wrappers around AI research pipelines requires
instrumentation at every step - not just at the point of publication.

Source: arXiv:2604.11261 (2026).

## Global Reporting Standards - International Science Council Initiative

**Source**: International Science Council (ISC). (2026). *AI Disclosure in
Research: Towards a Global Reporting Standard*.
https://council.science/our-work/ai-disclosure-in-research/

### The Problem with Current Disclosure

Current AI disclosure practices in research are:
- **Self-attested**: The author declares what AI was used with no verification
- **Coarse-grained**: "We used AI for writing assistance" tells readers almost
  nothing
- **Inconsistent across journals**: Some require disclosure; many do not
- **Not standardized**: No common vocabulary or taxonomy

### The Initiative

A coalition of organizations - International Science Council, World Conference
on Research Integrity Foundation, Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE),
and others - is developing a **Global Reporting Standard for AI Disclosure**
with:

- **Structured taxonomy**: Classification of AI use (data collection, analysis,
  hypothesis generation, writing, peer review - each treated differently)
- **Granularity requirements**: Not just "AI was used" but "AI model X was used
  for task Y, with human review at step Z"
- **Verification mechanisms**: How to check that disclosure is accurate
- **Analogy to CRediT taxonomy**: The Contributor Roles Taxonomy (CRediT)
  standardized author contribution statements; this initiative aims to do the
  same for AI contributions

**Status**: Consultation rounds scheduled through end of 2026. Expected
finalization late 2026 or 2027.

Source: https://council.science/our-work/ai-disclosure-in-research/

## The Accountability Gap

A fundamental difference between human and AI science that no current framework
fully addresses:

**In human science**: The author's name is on the paper. Their career depends
on its accuracy. False claims have consequences - retraction, loss of grants,
damage to reputation. This is not perfect (fraud exists) but it creates
structural accountability.

**In AI science**: The model's name is on the paper (if disclosed). The model
has no career, no reputation, no consequences. The authors who deployed the
model may be shielded by "the AI generated this." Accountability is diffuse.

### Three Models for Accountability Assignment

1. **Tool model**: AI is a tool; human author is fully accountable for all
   AI-generated content (current informal consensus). Simple but ignores that
   AI generation is fundamentally different from tool-use.

2. **Co-author model**: AI is listed as co-author; this requires formal
   frameworks for what authorship means for non-human agents. Most journals
   currently prohibit this (Nature, Science, JAMA policies).

3. **Process model**: Accountability attaches to the verification process,
   not the generation process. The human who verified the AI's claim is
   accountable for that claim. This requires traceable verification (see
   DeepReviewer 2.0 above).

The process model is most consistent with how replication-based accountability
works in science generally, but requires infrastructure that does not yet exist
at scale.

## Synthesis: Requirements for Epistemically Trustworthy AI Science

| Requirement | Current status | Solution direction |
|---|---|---|
| Citation accuracy | 40–80% in current systems | DeepTRACE auditing; citation verification pipelines |
| Claim-evidence traceability | Uniformly low | Research Object paradigm; DeepReviewer 2.0 |
| Disclosure standards | Inconsistent, self-attested | ISC Global Reporting Standard (2026+) |
| Accountability assignment | Unresolved | Process model; verification accountability |
| Replication infrastructure | Non-existent for AI science | Research Object reproducibility requirements |
| Hallucination detection | Benchmark-level tools only | Domain-specific verification agents |
| Provenance capture | Not built into AI pipelines | Instrumented AI research pipelines |
